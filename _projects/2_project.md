Visual object recognition depends on bidirectional information flow along the ventral visual stream — feedforward projections carrying sensory input and feedback projections conveying top-down predictions. Isolating these two signals in the human brain, both in space and time, has been a longstanding challenge.

In this project we combined **layer-specific 7T fMRI** with **time-resolved EEG** to dissociate feedforward and feedback signals at submillimeter cortical depth resolution. Feedforward signals emerged early (~80–120 ms) in the **middle cortical layers** of early visual cortex (V1/V2) and lateral occipital complex (LOC), consistent with driving inputs arriving via thalamocortical afferents. Feedback signals appeared later in the **superficial layers** of LOC, in line with the anatomy of long-range corticocortical feedback.

Benchmarking against deep neural network models revealed that early feedforward representations encoded **medium-to-high complexity features**, while later feedback representations carried **high-complexity object-level features** — suggesting that feedback actively shapes and enriches visual representations in support of recognition.

## Methods

- Layer-resolved BOLD fMRI at 7 Tesla (0.8 mm isotropic)
- Time-resolved EEG during rapid visual presentation
- Representational similarity analysis (RSA) against CNN model hierarchies
- Participants: human volunteers viewing naturalistic object images
