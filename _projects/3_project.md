How does the brain reconstruct a visual experience in the absence of external input? Visual imagery and perception both activate high-level visual regions, but the cortical mechanisms differ. Because feedforward input arrives via middle cortical layers while feedback from higher areas targets superficial and deep layers, **cortical depth profiles** offer a unique window into which process dominates a given activation.

Using **lamina-resolved fMRI at 7T** we measured BOLD responses at three cortical depths across the **fusiform face area (FFA)** and the **parahippocampal place area (PPA)** — two regions known to be active during both viewing and imagining faces and scenes, respectively.

During **perception**, activation was distributed relatively uniformly across all cortical layers, consistent with combined feedforward drive and top-down modulation. During **imagery**, activation concentrated in the **deep cortical layers**, reflecting purely internally generated feedback signals in the absence of any bottom-up sensory input.

These findings provide direct mesoscale evidence that visual imagery is implemented via top-down feedback mechanisms and suggest a laminar dissociation between externally and internally driven visual processing.

## Methods

- Lamina-resolved BOLD fMRI at 7 Tesla (0.8 mm isotropic)
- Fusiform face area and parahippocampal place area regions of interest
- Cortical depth sampling (superficial / middle / deep layers)
- Within-subject paradigm: perception vs. imagery of faces and places
